How Much Protein Do You Really Need?

Published Jul 8, 2026·7 min read

On this page

Protein recommendations range from a bare-minimum 0.8 g/kg to well over 2 g/kg depending on who you ask — and most of that confusion comes from mixing up two very different questions: how much protein prevents deficiency, and how much protein is optimal for someone training and trying to change their body composition.

The basics

The commonly cited 0.8 g/kg figure is the Recommended Dietary Allowance, calculated to prevent deficiency in a sedentary adult. It was never meant to describe an optimal intake for someone lifting weights, running, or trying to lose fat while keeping muscle. Sports nutrition research consistently lands on higher numbers for anyone training regularly.

Targets by goal

  • Maintenance, moderately active: roughly 1.2–1.6 g/kg
  • Muscle gain: roughly 1.6–2.2 g/kg
  • Fat loss while preserving muscle: roughly 1.8–2.4 g/kg
  • Body recomposition: often the highest of the four

Use the calculator on this site to turn any of these ranges into an exact gram target for your own body weight.

Does age change it?

Yes. Research on older adults points to a phenomenon sometimes called anabolic resistance — muscle tissue becomes less responsive to a given dose of protein with age. Many researchers suggest adults over 60 aim for the higher end of the maintenance range.

Best protein sources

Complete sources include eggs, dairy, poultry, fish, and soy. Plant sources like beans, lentils, and grains are still valuable when eaten in variety across the day.

The takeaway

There's no single correct number — only a range that depends on your training status, goal, and age. Start with the calculator, hold that target steady for a few weeks, and adjust based on how your body responds.
